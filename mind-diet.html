The short version

The MIND diet — short for Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay — was developed by nutritional epidemiologist Dr. Martha Clare Morris and colleagues as a hybrid of the Mediterranean and DASH diets. It emphasizes leafy greens, other vegetables, berries, nuts, beans, whole grains, olive oil, and fish, while keeping red meat, fried food, and sweets low.

Unlike diets defined by rules for a single day or a single plate, the MIND diet describes a weekly pattern: how often the encouraged foods show up in your week, and how rarely the limited ones do.

The evidence is mixed by study type. In observational studies, higher MIND diet scores have been associated with slower cognitive decline and lower dementia risk. In a three-year randomized trial, cognitive scores improved modestly in both groups, with no advantage for the MIND diet over a comparison diet — and analyses of that trial are ongoing.

The fourteen food groups

The MIND score counts fourteen food groups. Nine are encouraged:

  • Green leafy vegetables — spinach, kale, salad greens
  • Other vegetables
  • Berries — the one fruit group scored on its own
  • Nuts
  • Beans and other legumes
  • Whole grains
  • Fish (not fried)
  • Poultry (not fried)
  • Olive oil as the primary cooking oil

Five are limited:

  • Red meat and processed meat
  • Butter and margarine
  • Cheese
  • Pastries and sweets
  • Fried and fast food

Each group scores 0, 0.5, or 1 point against a weekly cutoff from the original research, and the points sum to a total. The original 2015 score also included wine as a fifteenth component; Vittelio deliberately leaves it out and scores 0–14.

Questions

Frequently asked questions

What does MIND stand for?

MIND stands for Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay — a hybrid of the Mediterranean and DASH diets, developed by epidemiologist Dr. Martha Clare Morris and colleagues.

How is the MIND diet different from the Mediterranean diet?

It borrows from both but scores some foods differently — green leafy vegetables and berries count as their own groups, and butter, cheese, sweets, red meat, and fried food are limited.

How do I start the MIND diet?

Measure the week you already eat and change a few meals at a time. Vittelio scores your week from 0 to 14 and suggests same-meal swaps. Talk to a health professional before changing your diet.
